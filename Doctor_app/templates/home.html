Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurological disorder that affects movement and coordination. It occurs due to the gradual loss of dopamine-producing neurons in a region of the brain called the substantia nigra. Dopamine is a critical neurotransmitter that helps regulate smooth, controlled movements. As dopamine levels drop, individuals experience symptoms like tremors, stiffness, slowness of movement, and difficulty with balance and coordination.

Certain genetic mutations are linked to the disease, while exposure to toxins like pesticides and heavy metals has been associated with an increased risk. However, no specific trigger has been definitively identified.

While there is currently no cure for Parkinson's, treatments can help manage its symptoms. Medications like levodopa help replenish dopamine levels in the brain, and other therapies, such as deep brain stimulation, may offer relief for advanced cases. In addition to medication, physical therapy and lifestyle changes can improve mobility and quality of life.
